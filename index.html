Global Warming: What is it?



Global warming is a phenomenon that occurs due to the increasingly rising temperatures created by human activity. Global warming can include increasing sea levels, warmer days, more weather-related natural disasters and it can be the cause of the extinction of several animal species. Global warming, also called climate change is caused by gas molecules known as greenhouse gases. These gases are released into the atmosphere due to mass pollution from burning fossil fuels, such as oil, petrol and coal. Once in the atmosphere, the gases begin trapping the sun's infrared light by absorbing the solar radiation reflected off the earth. As their name suggests, greenhouse gases act as earth's glass pane, restraining heat from escaping, but letting it in, hence the scientific term: greenhouse gas. We can prevent the effects of greenhouse gases by limiting and even eliminating the use of fossil fuels in human activities. This may seem like a problem but there already are existing solutions that could replace our usage of fossil fuels. The best alternative would be sources of renewable energy like; solar panels, wind turbines, hydropower and plenty other solutions. However, these options are more expensive and would costmillions of dollars to get rid of generators that use fossil fuels and to switch them out for generators that use renewable energy.

Endangered Species



The rapidly changing temperatures are a large problem since some living things cannot adapt as quickly as others; species are going extinct, and currently, there are 7000 endangered species, including fauna and flora. Species such as the Hawksbill Turtle, the Great Hammerhead, the Black Rhino, the Whale Shark and the Sunda Tiger. However, the list is extensive and are many others that are on the verge of extinction. Drastic measures need to be taken, and we need to protect these species as they each play a vital role in the sustentation of their ecosystems. When plants and animals become endangered, it is a signal that their ecosystems are slowly degrading; we rely heavily on certain ecosystems for purification, food and oxygen.

Purification



Ecosystems of the world are responsible for purifying our oxygen but also our water. Wetlands such as the Amazon absorb a large percentage of the sediments and pollutants in our water. The soil and flora of ecosystems aid in the filtration of these fine particles leaving us with cleaner water down the line. An example of an ecosystem at work is beaver dams; beaver dams have been observed to absorb 1000 times more nitrogen compared to no dams. Engineers have been copying this design to protect water streams against excess quantities of nitrogen which stimulates aquatic plant and algae growth; the invasive and parasitic nature of algae harms aquatic life and degrades ecosystems. The slowing of water by beaver-dams allows bacteria to react with the nitrates to produce nitrogen gas, for without the relatively still currents, there is no time for the process to occur.
